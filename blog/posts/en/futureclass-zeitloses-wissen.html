Timeless knowledge, trained AI-native: why I built FutureClass

NW Nils Weiser •16 JUL 2026 •—

1936: "How to Win Friends and Influence People." 1532: "The Prince." 1895: "The Crowd." 1926: "The Richest Man in Babylon." Four books, four eras, four completely different authors — and anyone who reads them makes the same discovery: they describe exactly the people we sit across from in meetings today.

The constant

Human nature has not fundamentally changed. What changed is only the garment — the words, the environment, the tools. Machiavelli wrote about princely courts, but the power dynamics he dissects play out in every boardroom today. Le Bon wrote about street crowds, but his crowd psychology explains social media dynamics better than most modern analyses. Carnegie wrote for traveling salesmen of the 1930s, and his core insight — people are interested first and foremost in themselves — is half the explanation for why that last sales conversation failed.

You can go back as far as you like. Aristotle broke persuasion down into ethos, pathos, and logos — credibility, emotion, argument. That is not a historical footnote; it is the skeleton of every working sales call in 2026. The ancient Greeks knew no less about people than we do today. They just had different examples.

Reading is not mastery

If this knowledge has been sitting openly in books for centuries — why do so few people master it?

Because reading is not mastery.

You read Carnegie, nodding on every page — and in the next meeting you still talk only about yourself. You understand negotiation psychology in theory — and in your next salary conversation you cave within two minutes. That is not a character flaw. It is the perfectly normal gap between declarative knowledge and procedural skill. Nobody learns to swim from a book.

Books deliver the what and the why. What they cannot deliver: repetition, feedback, and pressure — the three things skill is made of.

The missing training room

The people who truly master this knowledge — top negotiators, outstanding salespeople, leaders people gladly follow — did not just read it. They had training rooms: hundreds of real conversations, mentors, expensive failures. That training room has been a privilege. Role-plays need partners. Coaches cost money. And in real life, every failed attempt costs reputation, deals, or relationships.

The AI-native approach

This is exactly where FutureClass comes in — the platform I have built over the past months, live now.

FutureClass does not reinvent the knowledge. That would be presumptuous — and unnecessary. What FutureClass changes is the acquisition: practice-first, with AI.

Every lesson ends not with a quiz but in an AI Practice Lab: a realistic scenario where you train what you have learned against an AI that plays back. The customer who says no. The counterpart who stays skeptical. The negotiation that tips. You can run the scenario ten times, fail ten times, get feedback ten times — and it costs nothing but time.

At launch there are five courses — from winning people over to negotiation, sales, and hospitality — in English, German, Spanish, and French.

One important point: the books I name above are not the FutureClass curriculum. They are the proof that this knowledge is timeless. The curriculum is an original distillation of these principles — the training is the product.

Why I built this

I build AI agents for a living and work on AI security. FutureClass is my answer to a simple question: if AI can simulate any conversation today — why are we still training the most important skill of all, dealing with people, only by reading?

Timeless knowledge. AI-native methodology.

FutureClass is live: futureclass.io
